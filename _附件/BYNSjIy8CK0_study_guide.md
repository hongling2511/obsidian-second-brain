## 1. Overview of the Investor's Profile and Performance

The investor "Feng Sheng Shui Qi" is recognized as a legendary figure in the Chinese A-share market, transitioning from a humble background to a high-net-worth individual through disciplined value investing.

*   **Background:** Born in rural Hubei, he was a top student in the liberal arts, eventually graduating from Fudan University with a major in International Trade.
*   **Early Career:** He began trading in 1997 with 2,000 RMB. In 2001, after three years of work at a state-owned enterprise in Qingdao, he resigned to become a full-time investor with 160,000 RMB in capital.
*   **Record of Success:** Between 2004 and 2014, he achieved a 163-fold return on investment, representing a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 66%. By 2015, his assets were estimated to be over 100 million RMB, likely reaching 100 million USD in subsequent years.
*   **Methodology:** He identifies as a value investor, focusing on deep research into a limited number of stocks, tracking valuations, and maintaining long-term positions.

## 2. Core Investment Philosophy

The strategy is characterized by a "top-down" approach within specific sectors, described metaphorically as "choosing good students in good schools."

### Sector Focus
The investor restricts his activity to two primary "sunrise" or "evergreen" industries:
1.  **Electronic Information:** Leveraging his professional background in the industry.
2.  **Medical and Biological Sciences:** Focusing on innovation and long-term demand.

### Buying and Selling Principles
*   **Entry:** Buy when the company is undervalued.
*   **Holding:** Maintain long-term positions while continuously monitoring for overvaluation.
*   **Exit:** Selling is based on a **relative standard** rather than an absolute one. He exits when:
    *   The stock becomes significantly overvalued.
    *   A clearly superior opportunity arises (e.g., a high-growth new stock appearing during a bear market with a low valuation).

## 3. Stock Selection: The 10 Mandatory Criteria

The investor adheres to ten "must-have" conditions. If a company violates even one of these, it is excluded from consideration.

| No. | Criterion | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Focus on Core Business** | Avoid companies with blind diversification (e.g., Evergrande, LeEco). Diversification often signals a loss of focus or an attempt to fund expansion through risky means. |
| **2** | **Prominent Industry Status** | The company must have strong core competitiveness and pricing power over suppliers and customers. |
| **3** | **Increasing Net Profit** | There must be a clear trend of year-on-year profit growth, backed by a sustainable competitive advantage. |
| **4** | **Shareholder Returns** | High regard for companies that return value through dividends and buybacks. Historical data shows long-term "bull stocks" are often those that return the most to shareholders. |
| **5** | **Controlled Expansion** | Expansion should not be overly aggressive. Rapid expansion (e.g., "driving at 200 mph") increases the risk of a sudden collapse. |
| **6** | **Sound Governance** | Avoid companies plagued by internal strife, family disputes, or complicated state-private conflicts that might harm minority shareholders. |
| **7** | **Avoid Personal Heroism** | The company should not rely solely on a "lone wolf" or "gambler" leader. While some leaders are famous (e.g., Musk), sustainable companies have professional board structures. |
| **8** | **Continuous Innovation** | This includes not just technology, but also innovations in business models and management styles to find new growth points. |
| **9** | **Reasonable Valuation** | Avoid stocks where the price has "overdrawn" future earnings (e.g., P/E ratios over 100). |
| **10** | **High Integrity** | A zero-tolerance policy for dishonesty. If a company lies once, it is "blacklisted" forever. |

## 4. The 5 Preferred Standards (Optimization)

Beyond the mandatory rules, these standards help identify the best possible candidates for high returns:

1.  **Abundant Cash Flow:** Profits must be "real" cash, not just accounting figures. Beware of companies with high accounts receivable (common in manufacturing supply chains).
2.  **Good Industry Prospects:** The sector must have a clear future (sunrise industries).
3.  **High Concentration of "Chips":** Presence of large institutional investors. This usually leads to lower volatility and prevents "pump and dump" schemes by hot money (Yuzhi).
4.  **Management Buybacks/Holding Increases:** When executives buy their own stock, it signals confidence in future operations and an assessment that the stock is cheap.
5.  **Small to Moderate Scale:** To achieve high returns (e.g., 10x), one must invest in "infant" or "adolescent" companies rather than mature giants.

## 7. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **519 Market (519 行情):** A significant historical bull market rally in the Chinese A-share market (starting May 19, 1999).
*   **Sunrise Industry (朝阳行业):** An industry that is new or growing fast, expected to become important in the future (e.g., Electronic Information, Innovation Drugs).
*   **Relative Standard (相对标准):** A selling philosophy where the decision to exit a position is based on the emergence of a better opportunity elsewhere, rather than just hitting a specific price target.
*   **Chips Concentration (筹码集中度):** A measure of how much of a company's stock is held by a few large entities (institutions) versus many small retail investors.
*   **Overdrawing Future Earnings (透支未来):** A situation where a stock's current price is so high that it already accounts for many years of future growth, leaving little room for further appreciation.
*   **Personal Heroism (个人英雄主义):** A management style centered entirely on the charisma or decisions of a single leader, which the investor views as a long-term risk.
*   **Hot Money / Yuzhi (游资):** Short-term, speculative capital that seeks quick profits through high volatility and "hype," often avoiding stocks held by large institutions.
