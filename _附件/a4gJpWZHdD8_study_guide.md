# Comprehensive Investment Study Guide: Enterprise Evaluation and Valuation Strategies

This study guide synthesizes the investment philosophies and operational strategies of the renowned investor known as "Feng Sheng Shui Qi." It focuses on the rigorous assessment of corporate strength, the nuances of valuation, and the tactical management of a portfolio within the A-share market.

---

## Part 1: Core Principles of Enterprise Evaluation

The following five principles form the foundation for assessing whether a company possesses the requisite strength for long-term investment.

### 1. Product Nature and the Assembly Trap
Investors should exercise caution regarding manufacturers of products that are overly integrated into daily life if those companies act merely as assemblers. 
*   **The Risk:** Assembly-focused firms often lack core technology, leaving them with minimal bargaining power against upstream component suppliers.
*   **The Exception:** This rule is flexible if the company has "broken through" and internalized the production of key components (e.g., air conditioner manufacturers who produce their own compressors).

### 2. Profit Margin Thresholds
Profitability is a primary indicator of a company's survival and growth potential.
*   **The 30% Rule:** A gross profit margin (GPM) below 30% often indicates a company is struggling on the "survival line," merely stacking scale without generating real value.
*   **The Strategy Ladder:**
    *   **30%+ GPM:** Necessary to keep pace with industry iterations.
    *   **40%+ GPM:** Required for companies aiming to build robust sales channels.
    *   **45-50%+ GPM:** Required to sustain high-end brand building and consumer mindshare.

### 3. Management Incentives and Efficiency
The alignment of management interests with shareholders is critical.
*   **Equity Incentives:** Modern investors should look for well-structured equity incentive plans, which became standard in the Chinese market around 2014–2015.
*   **Human Efficiency (Per Capita Revenue):** A key metric is the revenue generated per employee. If a company's per-capita revenue is too low (e.g., 200,000–300,000 RMB), the gross profit may not even cover salaries and social security, leaving nothing for shareholders.

### 4. Geographic and Regional Factors
Investment "boundaries" often exist based on regional marketization and transparency.
*   **Talent Hubs:** Technology and electronic information firms should ideally be located in talent-dense regions like Beijing, Shenzhen, Hangzhou, or Shanghai.
*   **Regional Risks:** Certain regions have historically been associated with higher rates of financial fraud or "zombie" listings due to provincial quota systems.

### 5. Historical Dividend Consistency
Dividends are the ultimate proof of "real" profit.
*   **The 30% Payout:** A healthy enterprise should be willing to return approximately 30% of its annual profit to shareholders.
*   **Growth in Dividends:** For a growth stock, the most important indicator is not just the earnings growth, but the consistent growth of the dividend payout.

## Part 2: Valuation Methodologies

Valuation is described as a blend of "science" (understanding financial statements) and "art" (determining what a business is worth in the current moment).

### The PE and PEG Framework
*   **PE Ratio (Price-to-Earnings):** A universal metric. Note that even legendary investors like Warren Buffett typically target entries at 15–17x PE.
*   **PEG Ratio (PE divided by Growth Rate):** A 30x PE is considered reasonable if the projected earnings growth rate is also 30%.
*   **High PE Logic:** A 100x PE stock can be a viable investment if it doubles its earnings annually. Within two years, the valuation effectively drops to 25x if the price remains stagnant, often leading to a massive "catch-up" rally.

### Industry-Specific Scalability
Valuations must account for how quickly a business can scale.
*   **Manufacturing:** Scaling requires physical factories, government approvals, and worker training, often taking 2–3 years to double capacity.
*   **Finance and Internet:** These industries can scale almost instantly. A fund manager can manage 500 million as easily as 100 million; an app can go from 1 million to 5 million users overnight. Consequently, these industries often command higher valuation tolerances.

### The Scale Hierarchy in A-Shares
The market's perception of a company changes as its market capitalization grows:
*   **2B to 5B RMB:** Driven largely by concepts and market themes.
*   **5B to 10B RMB:** Requires actual profit generation and industry effort.
*   **10B to 50B RMB:** The company must emerge as a "Dragon Head" (industry leader) or a "Hidden Champion" in a niche sector.
*   **Above 50B RMB:** The market becomes skeptical of the "ceiling," often leading to valuation suppression (killing the PE) unless growth remains extraordinary.

## Part 3: Operational Strategies

### The "Sun and Planet" Portfolio Model
This strategy balances stability with aggressive growth:
*   **The Sun (Core Holdings):** Large, stable "long cows" with steady performance and reasonable valuations. These are held for the long term.
*   **The Planets (Satellite Holdings):** Faster-moving stocks, perhaps with higher uncertainty but multi-bagger potential.
*   **The Mechanism:** Profits harvested from "Planet" stocks (short-term swings or mid-term cycles) should not be kept as cash but reinvested into "Sun" stocks to maximize the power of compound interest.

### Tactical Entry and Exit
*   **Avoid the "Eye-Catcher":** Do not buy when a stock is dominating headlines with massive volume and high turnover.
*   **The 90-Degree Rule:** If a stock’s rise steepens from a sustainable 60-degree angle to a vertical 90-degree "sprint," prepare to sell in batches.
*   **The December Switch:** December is a critical month for "valuation switching," where the market begins pricing stocks based on the following year's earnings. This month often presents opportunities for the "Spring Movement" (a seasonal rally).

## Part 6: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dragon Head (Long Tou)** | The absolute market leader in a specific industry or sector, typically commanding the highest valuation and stability. |
| **PEG Ratio** | A valuation metric determined by dividing the PE ratio by the annual earnings growth rate. A PEG of 1.0 is generally considered a fair balance between price and growth. |
| **Valuation Switching** | The period (typically year-end) when the market stops valuing a company based on the current year’s earnings and begins valuing it based on projected earnings for the next year. |
| **Spring Movement** | A recurring historical trend in the A-share market where stock prices rally in the early months of the year, often driven by renewed investor risk appetite. |
| **Per-Capita Efficiency** | A metric calculated by dividing total revenue by the number of official employees, used to determine if the business model is inherently profitable. |
| **Hidden Champion** | A company that is a market leader in a very specific, narrow niche, often with high profit margins but lower public visibility. |
| **Asset "Shower" (Restatements)** | A colloquial term for when a company suddenly writes off large amounts of bad debt or inventory, often to "clean" the books for future reporting. |
| **Capital Efficiency (ROI)** | The return an investor receives through the combination of stock price appreciation, dividends, and corporate buybacks. |
