# Global Economic Outlook 2026: Analysis and Investment Strategy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the global economic landscape leading into 2026, based on an expert panel discussion featuring Torsten Sløk (Apollo Global Management), Larry Hatheway (Franklin Templeton), and moderator William Lee. The guide focuses on the shifting paradigms of trade policy, labor supply, and the evolving structure of capital markets.

---

## I. The Economic Landscape: Headwinds and Tailwinds

The economic outlook for 2025–2026 is characterized by a "Nike swoosh" growth trajectory—a sharp slowdown followed by a gradual recovery. Analysts identify a specific set of forces shaping this transition.

### Primary Headwinds
Economists identify three critical factors currently slowing economic momentum:

*   **Tariff Policies:** The implementation of broad-based tariffs acts as a primary drag on the economy. These policies lead to higher import prices, which Walmart and other retailers note results in decreased sales volume. This puts downward pressure on GDP while simultaneously increasing inflation.
*   **Immigration Restrictions and Deportations:** Current administration goals involve the deportation of approximately 3,000 people daily, totaling roughly one million people per year. Given a population of 12 million unauthorized immigrants, this reduction in the labor supply is expected to lower employment growth and increase wage pressure in specific sectors, including:
    *   Construction
    *   Agriculture
    *   Hotels and Restaurants
*   **Student Loan Repayments:** The restart of student loan payments (following the pandemic-era pause) reduces disposable consumer income. Additionally, missed payments negatively impact credit scores, making it more difficult for consumers to secure loans for major purchases like cars and homes.

### Secondary Tailwinds
While present, these factors are considered less forceful than the prevailing headwinds:
*   High stock prices and tight credit spreads.
*   Anticipated Federal Reserve interest rate cuts.
*   A recent downward trend in the valuation of the U.S. dollar.

## II. The Stagflation Forecast and Fiscal Risks

A unique and "highly unusual" aspect of the current consensus forecast is the expectation of **stagflation**—a period characterized by slowing growth paired with rising inflation.

### The Macroeconomic Forecast (Consensus Data)
| Metric | Historical Trend | 2025–2026 Forecast |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Real GDP Growth** | High (2.4% – 3.1%) | Slowdown to 1.1% – 1.9% |
| **Inflation** | ~2.5% | Rising to 2.9% – 3.1% |
| **Recession Probability** | Variable | 30% (Consensus View) |

### Fiscal Concerns and Treasury Issuance
The U.S. fiscal situation remains a structural risk. Debt-to-GDP ratios are expected to rise, creating concerns regarding the sustainability of government spending.
*   **Issuance Trends:** Treasury issuance is currently high for short-term and mid-term rates (2, 3, and 5-year notes) but remains limited at the long end of the curve (20 and 30-year bonds).
*   **Foreign Investment:** Foreigners have shown a shifting appetite for U.S. assets. While they remain buyers of equities (driven by the AI narrative), there is growing hesitation regarding government bonds due to fiscal instability.

## III. Trade Policy: From Tariffs to Investment Strategy

The discussion highlights a fundamental shift in how trade policy is utilized. Rather than merely being an "expenditure-reducing" tool, current policies are viewed as "expenditure-switching" and investment-focused.

### The "Trade War with Everyone"
The U.S. approach differs from other nations (like South Korea) because it has initiated trade disputes with a broad range of countries simultaneously. This "universal" trade war contributes significantly to the U.S.-specific inflation risk, as price increases are not mitigated by trading with non-dispute partners.

### The Investment Incentive Model
The current administration views tariffs as an instrument of an "investment policy." The goal is to create a "tariff barrier" that incentivizes foreign competitors to become partners by:
1.  Moving production facilities to the United States.
2.  Bringing capital and technology into the domestic market.
3.  Creating local jobs to avoid high import duties.

## IV. Evolving Market Structures: Public vs. Private

The financial landscape is undergoing a structural shift as the number of public companies in the U.S. has dropped from roughly 8,000 to 4,000.

### The Rise of Private Markets
Investors, particularly pension funds and insurance companies, are increasingly moving toward **private credit** and **private equity**.
*   **Private Credit:** Offers yields typically 100 to 200 basis points higher than public markets. It serves as a "fixed income replacement" for investors who do not require daily liquidity.
*   **The Endowment Model:** This model has faced challenges recently. As interest rates rose, the high allocation of private equity in university endowments (like Harvard and Yale) faced a higher bar for returns, leading to fewer exits and distributions.

### The Three-Dimensional Portfolio
Traditional portfolio construction focused on a two-dimensional trade-off: **Risk vs. Return**. Experts now argue for a three-dimensional model that optimizes for:
1.  **Risk** (Volatility)
2.  **Return**
3.  **Liquidity** (The ability to access cash during market drawdowns).

## V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What are the three primary economic headwinds identified by Torsten Sløk?**
    *Answer:* Tariffs, immigration restrictions/deportations, and the restart of student loan payments.
2.  **How do immigration restrictions specifically impact inflation?**
    *Answer:* They reduce the labor supply, which leads to lower employment growth and puts upward pressure on wages, particularly in sectors like construction and agriculture.
3.  **What is the "Nike swoosh" growth forecast?**
    *Answer:* A forecast where growth slows down significantly in the near term (2025) and then begins a gradual improvement into 2026.
4.  **Why is the U.S. inflation outlook described as "unique" compared to Korea?**
    *Answer:* Because the U.S. is engaged in a trade war with almost everyone simultaneously, whereas Korea maintains trade without price increases with most other nations.
5.  **According to Larry Hatheway, what is the "third dimension" investors must now consider in portfolio construction?**
    *Answer:* Liquidity.
6.  **What has happened to the number of public companies in the U.S. over recent decades?**
    *Answer:* The number has shrunk by approximately half, from roughly 8,000 to 4,000.

## VII. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Alpha:** The excess return of an investment relative to the return of a benchmark index.
*   **Basis Points (bps):** A unit of measure for interest rates and other percentages in finance (100 basis points = 1%).
*   **Expenditure Switching:** A policy intended to shift consumer spending from foreign-produced goods to domestically produced goods (e.g., via tariffs).
*   **Fiscal Deficit:** The gap between a government's total expenditures and its total receipts (excluding money from borrowings).
*   **Goldilocks Economy:** An economic state characterized by high growth and low inflation—neither "too hot" nor "too cold."
*   **Lucas Paradox:** The economic observation that capital does not always flow from rich countries to poor countries (where it is scarce), but often flows toward countries with higher returns and innovation.
*   **Nike Swoosh Recovery:** A recovery pattern characterized by a sharp decline followed by a long, gradual increase back toward previous levels.
*   **Private Credit:** Debt financing provided by non-bank institutions to private companies.
*   **Stagflation:** A period of stagnant economic growth combined with high inflation and high unemployment.
*   **Tight Credit Spreads:** A condition where the difference in yield between different debt instruments (like corporate bonds vs. treasuries) is small, indicating low perceived risk in the market.
