# Investment Philosophy and Bear Market Navigation: The Strategies of Fengsheng Shuiqi

This study guide synthesizes the investment principles, core methodologies, and bear market survival strategies utilized by the investor known as "Fengsheng Shuiqi." It focuses on the transition from amateur to professional investing, the importance of risk management, and the psychological discipline required to maintain a long-term value-oriented perspective.

---

## I. The Ten Essential Investment Principles

A mature investor is defined by their adherence to strict behavioral and analytical codes. The following ten principles form the foundation of this investment philosophy:

1.  **Prioritize Risk Over Gain:** Before entering any trade, the investor must first calculate the potential loss they are willing to accept. Amateurs often chase "hot" stocks and short-term gains, whereas mature investors focus on the downside.
2.  **Abstain from Stock "Diagnosis":** Avoid providing casual evaluations of stock price fluctuations. Focus instead on the underlying business logic. Analyze the "business" rather than the "movement."
3.  **Maintain Portfolio Privacy:** Do not disclose specific investment holdings to friends or the public. Public exposure creates psychological pressure to "prove oneself right," which hinders the ability to remain objective and correct mistakes as market fundamentals change.
4.  **Refusal of Entrusted Management:** Managing funds for others introduces immense pressure regarding drawdowns and client expectations. Professional asset management often forces investors into sub-optimal decisions (like "chasing highs and killing lows") due to liquidation lines (typically at 0.7 or 0.8) and client impatience.
5.  **Avoid Becoming a "Slave to the Market":** Constant monitoring of every news cycle and policy change can lead to panic and selling at market bottoms. Investors should maintain a distance from the market to preserve a "peaceful mind" (平常心).
6.  **Avoid the Crowd:** Do not participate in investment behaviors or sectors where the majority of the market is already active. When a niche insight becomes mainstream and a stock becomes "hot," the original valuation advantage has likely vanished.
7.  **Objective and Rigorous Analysis:** Treat stocks as businesses, not lovers. Avoid subjective narratives that justify holding a stock during a downturn. This is particularly important for cyclical stocks, which investors often mistake for growth stocks at the peak of a cycle.
8.  **Prudence in Public Commentary:** Maintain high standards and caution when commenting on market trends or stocks in public forums or blogs.
9.  **Attribute Failure to Self and Success to Luck:** Success is often a product of favorable timing (e.g., the "Golden Years" of the A-share market) or institutional advantages (e.g., shell value systems for small-cap stocks). Recognizing the role of luck prevents overconfidence.
10. **Independent Thinking:** Do not blindly follow "experts" or dismiss "novices." Information can come from anywhere; an industry insider might be a "novice" in the stock market but possess critical, high-value information about their specific sector.

## II. The Three Iron Laws of Investment

Beyond general principles, three "Iron Laws" govern the execution of trades and long-term strategy:

| Law | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Awe the Market** | Never bet against market bubbles or assume they will expand indefinitely. Stick to common sense: stock price growth must eventually align with earnings growth (Mean Reversion). |
| **Focus on the Familiar** | Operate strictly within a "Circle of Competence." Deeply research specific industries rather than skimming surface-level news. Professionalism comes from knowing when *not* to play unless a "water fish" (inexperienced participant) is at the table. |
| **Independent Operation** | Buy and sell based on independent logic. A fundamental rule is: **"Sell for the same reason you bought."** If you bought for speculation, sell when the speculative event ends; do not use "value" as an excuse to hold a losing speculative trade. |

## III. Strategies for Navigating Bear Markets

To "cross" or survive a bear market while remaining profitable, the following criteria must be met:

*   **Valuation Advantage:** The chosen stock must have a clear valuation edge over its peers. It should ideally be the "last to fall" during a market-wide decline.
*   **Guaranteed Performance:** There must be a high probability of earnings growth over the next 1–2 years. This requires analyzing differentiated products, market space, and the likelihood of those products reaching their "ceiling."
*   **"Catch the Small, Release the Large":** In bear markets, institutional funds are scarce. Large-cap stocks (often used for index matching) are difficult to move. Small-cap stocks require less liquidity and are more likely to present independent opportunities.
*   **New Over Old:** Focus on newly listed companies for growth. In the A-share context, growth often comes from companies entering new cycles or sectors (e.g., the shift from consumer electronics to chips or new energy).
*   **Relative Strength (Resistance):** Identify stocks that remain resilient or even trend upward while the broader market is crashing. Research the fundamental reason for this resistance; if it is based on solid earnings or valuation, it is a prime entry point.

## IV. Portfolio Structure and Position Management

The philosophy advocates for a concentrated but diversified portfolio:
*   **Concentration:** Typically hold no more than **3 to 5 stocks**.
*   **Core Position:** A single "heavy" position (where research is deepest) may account for **30% to 50%** of the total portfolio.
*   **Sector Diversification:** While the portfolio is concentrated in the number of stocks, those stocks should span different industries to mitigate sector-specific risk (e.g., holding a mix of security hardware, software outsourcing, and medical instruments).

## VII. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Circle of Competence (能力圈):** The specific industries or types of stocks an investor understands deeply enough to have an information or analytical advantage.
*   **Safety Margin (安全边际):** The gap between the current market value and the liquidation value (Graham) or future estimated value (Buffett) that protects the investor from loss.
*   **Shell Value (壳价值):** The intrinsic value of a listed company’s status on the exchange, regardless of its business performance; historically significant in the A-share market for small-cap stocks.
*   **Drawdown (回撤):** The peak-to-trough decline in the value of an investment or portfolio during a specific period.
*   **Mean Reversion (均值回归):** The mathematical tendency for stock prices to eventually return to their long-term average or align with actual earnings.
*   **Insurance Float (保险浮存金):** Low-cost capital (typically associated with Buffett) that allows for long-term investment without the pressure of immediate withdrawal.
*   **Event-Driven (事件驱动):** An investment strategy based on predicting the outcome of a specific corporate or market event.
